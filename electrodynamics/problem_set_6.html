Problem 1: Larmor Radiation

The Larmor formula can be applied just as well to a magnetic dipole by simply replacing the electric dipole moment \(\vec{p}\) by the magnetic dipole moment \(\vec{m}\). Given that the Earth has a magnetic dipole moment with magnitude \( 8.1 \times 10^{25}\) ergs/Gauss and direction \(11^\circ\) to the Earth's axis of rotation, find the total power radiated in CGS units (ergs/sec). You will need the unit conversion (1 Gauss)\(^2\) = \(8\pi\) ergs/cm\(^3\).

Solution

The magnetic dipole moment can be written down in spherical coordinates as: \(\vec{m} = m_0\,\hat{n}(t)\) where \(\hat{n}(t)\) is the unit normal vector pointing in the direction of \(\vec{m}\). For our case, the earth is rotating (about its axis of rotation) so the polar angle is fixed and the azimuthal angle \(\varphi = \omega t\) is free to evolve where \(\omega = 2\pi/24\,\)hours. $$ \begin{align} \hat{n}(t) = \cos(\omega t) \sin(11^\circ) \hat{x} + \sin(\omega t) \sin(11^\circ) \hat{y} + \cos(11^\circ) \hat{z} \end{align} $$ Then differentiating this twice gives us $$ \begin{align} \dot{\hat{n}}(t) &= -\omega\sin(\omega t )\sin(11^\circ) \hat{x} + \omega\cos(\omega t)\sin(11^\circ)\hat{y}\\ \ddot{\hat{n}}(t) &= -\omega^2\cos(\omega t)\sin(11^\circ)\hat{x} - \omega^2 \sin(\omega t )\sin(11^\circ)\,\hat{y} \end{align} $$ Then the second derivative of the magnetic dipole moment becomes $$ \begin{align} \ddot{\vec{m}} = -m_0\omega^2 \sin(11^\circ) (\cos\omega t \hat{x} + \sin\omega t \hat{y}) \end{align} $$ Inserting this into the magnetic dipole moment and into the Larmor formula gives us $$ \begin{align} P = \frac{2}{3c^3}\ddot{\vec{m}} &= \frac{2m_0^2\omega^4}{3c^3} \left(\cos^2\omega t + \sin^2\omega t\right)\sin^2(11^\circ)\\ &= \frac{2m_0\omega^4}{3c^3}\sin^2(11^\circ) \end{align} $$ Then using \(m_0 = 8.1\times10^{25}\) ergs/Gauss, \(\omega = 2\pi/86400\) s\(^{-1}\), \(c = 3\times 10^{10}\) cm/s, we get $$ \begin{align} P &= 164.959 \,\frac{\text{ergs}^2}{\text{Gauss}^2\cdot \text{s}\cdot \text{cm}^3} = \frac{164.959}{8\pi} \, \frac{\text{ergs}}{\text{s}} = 6.564\, \frac{\text{ergs}}{\text{s}} \end{align} $$



Problem 2: Lienard-Wichert Potentials

A charged particle \(q\) traveling on the \(x\)-axis with a non-relativistic velocity approaches the origin from the left (ie, along the \(+\hat{x})\) direction). It decelerates at a constant rate \(a\) coming to a complete stop at the origin at \(t=0\).

Using the expressions for \(E_\text{rad}\) for a non-relativistic charge derived from the LW potentials, prove that the radiative part of the electirc field at the point (\(x = 0, y = b\)) at \(t=0\) is given by $$ E_x = \frac{qab^2}{c^2 R^3} $$ and $$ E_y = - \frac{qa^2 b}{2Rc^4} $$ where \( R = |\vec{r} - \vec{r}'(t')| \). Find an expression for \(R\) in terms of \(a,b\) and the speed of light \(c\) only.

Solution

The radiation part of the electric field, from the LW potentials, is given by $$ \begin{align} \vec{E}_\text{rad} &= \frac{q}{c} \left[\frac{\hat{n} \times [(\hat{n}-\vec{\beta} ) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}]}{(1-\vec{\beta}\cdot\hat{n})^3 R}\right]_{t=t'} \end{align} $$ where \(\hat{n}\) is a unit vector in the \(\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0\) direction (\(\vec{r}\) is the position of the observer, \(\vec{r}_0\) is the position of the source), \(\beta = d\vec{r}_0/dt\), \(R\) is the magnitude \(R = |\vec{r}-\vec{r}_0(t')|\), and \(t'\) is the retarded time. In the non-relativistic limit \(\beta \ll 1\) so this expression reduces to $$ \begin{align} \vec{E}_\text{rad} = \frac{q}{c} \left[\frac{\hat{n} \times [(\hat{n}-\vec{\beta} ) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}]}{R}\right]_{t=t'} \end{align} $$ We can start by writing down the various quantities.

  • First, we can find \(\hat{n} = \vec{R}/R = (\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0)/|\vec{r}-\vec{r}_0|\). \begin{align} \vec{R} = \vec{r} - \vec{r}_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 0\ b \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}at^2\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2}at^2\ b \end{pmatrix} \end{align} This then gives us \begin{align} R = \sqrt{(at^2/2)^2 + b^2} \end{align} which together gives us \begin{align} \hat{n} &= \frac{\frac{1}{2}at^2\hat{x} + b\hat{y}}{\sqrt{(at^2/2)^2 + b^2}} = \frac{1}{R}\left(\frac{1}{2}at^2\hat{x} + b\hat{y}\right) \end{align}
  • Next, we can write down \(\vec{\beta}\) and \(\dot{\vec{\beta}}\) $$ \begin{align} \vec{\beta} = \frac{\vec{v}}{c} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{d\vec{r}_0(t)}{dt} = \begin{pmatrix} - at/c\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$ and $$ \begin{align} \dot{\vec{\beta}} &= \begin{pmatrix} a/c\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$
At this point, we can start to calculate things. In the following steps, we aim to calculate the numerator inside of the brackets \(\hat{n}\times[(\hat{n} - \vec{\beta}) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}]\).
  1. First finding \(\hat{n} - \vec{\beta}\) $$ \begin{align} \hat{n} - \vec{\beta} &= \frac{1}{R}\left(\frac{1}{2}at^2\hat{x} + b\hat{y}\right) - (-at/c \hat{x} )\ &= \frac{1}{R} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2}at^2 + Rat/c\ b \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$
  2. Next, finding \( (\hat{n} \times \vec{\beta}) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}\) $$ \begin{align} (\hat{n} \times \vec{\beta}) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}} &= \frac{1}{R} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2}at^2 + Rat/c\ b \ 0 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} a/c\ 0\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = -\frac{ab}{Rc}\hat{z} \end{align} $$
  3. Finally getting \( \hat{n} \times [ (\hat{n} \times \vec{\beta}) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}]\) $$ \begin{align} \hat{n} \times [ (\hat{n} \times \vec{\beta}) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}] &= \frac{1}{R} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2}at^2\ b \ 0 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 0\ 0\ -\frac{ab}{Rc} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{R^2c} \begin{pmatrix} -ab^2\ \frac{1}{2}a^2bt^2\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$
Putting this all back together gives us the components of the radiation field as $$ \begin{align} \vec{E}_\text{rad} &= \frac{q}{c} \left[\frac{\hat{n} \times [(\hat{n}-\vec{\beta} ) \times \dot{\vec{\beta}}]}{R}\right]_{t=t'} = \frac{q}{R^3c^2} \begin{pmatrix} -ab^2\\ \frac{1}{2}a^2bt^2\\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \bigg|_{t=t'} \end{align} $$ Next, we can recall that these terms are evaluated at \(t =t'\) so using $$ \begin{align} t' = \cancel{t} - \frac{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}'(t')|}{c} \qquad \implies \qquad t' = - \frac{R}{c} \end{align} $$ we arrive at $$ \begin{align} \vec{E}_\text{rad} &= \frac{q}{R^3c^2} \begin{pmatrix} -ab^2\\ \frac{1}{2}a^2bR^2/c^2 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} $$ or in component form $$ \begin{align} \boxed{ E_x = -\frac{qab^2}{c^2 R^3} \qquad E_y = \frac{qa^2 b}{2Rc^4}} \end{align} $$

Expression for \(R\)

To find an expression for \(R\) in terms of \(a,b,c\), we can start from our current expression for \(R\) $$ \begin{align} R = |\vec{r} - \vec{r}'(t')| = \sqrt{\left(\frac{at'^2}{2}\right)^2 + b^2} \end{align} $$ Next, we'll solve for \(t'\) from the implicit equation defining the retarded time (at \( t = 0\)) $$ \begin{align} t' &= \cancel{t} - \frac{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'(t')|}{c} = - \frac{\sqrt{(at'^2/2)^2 + b^2}}{c} \end{align} $$ We can solve this for \(t'\) to find (using Mathematica) $$ \begin{align} t' &= \pm \sqrt{2}\left[\left(\frac{c}{a}\right)^2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{c^4 - a^2 b^2}}{a^2}\right]^{1/2} \end{align} $$ Then inserting this back into the expression for \(R\) we find $$ \begin{align} R &= \sqrt{b^2 + \frac{a^4}{4}\left(2\left[\left(\frac{c}{a}\right)^2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{c^4 - a^2 b^2}}{a^2}\right]\right)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{b^2 + a^4 \left[\left(\frac{c}{a}\right)^2 \pm \frac{\sqrt{c^4 - a^2 b^2}}{a^2}\right]^2} \end{align} $$

Problem 3: Cherenkov and Bremsstrahlung Radiation

  1. An electron zips by a stationary ion (charge \(Ze\)) traveling in a straight line with an impact parameter \(b > 0\). Given the formula for instantaneous power radiated by the electron (in CGS units) $$ P(t) = \frac{2}{3}\frac{e^2a_\perp(t)}{c^3} $$ where \(a_\perp\) is the instaneous veritcal component of the acceleration, show that in the approximation taht electron velocity is almost constant (ie energy loss is small), the total energy radiated away \(W = \int_0^\infty P(t)\,dt\) is $$ W = \frac{\pi Z^2 e^6}{4m_e^2 c^3}\left(\frac{1}{b^3 v}\right) $$
  2. The index of refraction of Lucite (a type of resin) is 1.5. What is the angle of emission of Cherenkov radiation from a protion moving in a slab of lucite with kinetic energy 3760 MeV? The mass of a proton is 940 MeV.

Solution

Part A

We can start by finding the perpendicular part of the acceleration.
  • First, we can get the total acceleration vector by using Coulomb's law and Newton's second law together. The perpendicular part in this case would be the \(y\) component. \begin{align} \vec{F} &= \frac{Ze^2}{r^2}(\cos\theta\hat{x} + \sin\theta\hat{y}) \qquad \implies \qquad a_\perp = \frac{\vec{F}_\perp}{m} = \frac{Ze^2}{mr^2}\sin\theta \end{align} Thinking ahead to when we perform the integral to get the total work, we would want to re-write \(r\) in terms of \(\theta\) \begin{align} r\sin\theta = b \qquad \implies \qquad r = \frac{b}{\sin\theta} \end{align} which gives \(a_\perp\) as \begin{align} a_\perp =\frac{Ze^2}{mb^2}\sin^3\theta \end{align}
Now that we have \(a_\perp\), we can insert it into the work integral $$ \begin{align} W &= \int_0^\infty P(t)\,dt = \int_0^\infty \frac{2e^2}{3c^3}\left(\frac{Ze^2}{mb^2}\sin^3\theta\right)^2\,dt \end{align} $$ But this is an integral over \(t\) which is a little inconvenient, so we can change variables to integrate over \(\theta\) rather than \(t\), ie, we would want to use \(dt = dt/d\theta \cdot d\theta\).
  • In particular, we can use the fact that the particle's velocity \(v\) is almost constant to write down its position in terms of \(v, b, t\) $$ \begin{align} \vec{r}(t) = v t\hat{x} + b\hat{y} \qquad \implies \qquad r(t) = \sqrt{v^2 t^2 + b^2} \end{align} $$ But we also have \( r = b/\sin\theta\) so equating the two expressions gives us $$ \begin{align} \frac{b}{\sin\theta} &= \sqrt{v^2t^2 + b^2} \end{align} $$ and solving for \(t\) gives us $$ \begin{align} v^2 t^2 + b^2 &= \left(\frac{b}{\sin\theta}\right)^2\ t^2 &= \frac{b^2}{v^2} \left[\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta} - 1\right] = \frac{b^2}{v^2}\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin^2\theta}\ t &= \pm \frac{b}{v}\frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} \end{align} $$ We can differentiate this with respect to \(\theta\) to give us $$ \begin{align} \frac{dt}{d\theta} &= \pm \frac{b}{v}\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta} \end{align} $$
With this result, our change-of-variables integral becomes $$ \begin{align} W &= \int_0^\pi \frac{2e^2}{3c^3}\left(\frac{Ze^2}{mb^2}\sin^3\theta\right)^2\,\left(\frac{b}{v}\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\right)\,d\theta\\ &= \frac{2e^6 Z^2}{3c^3 m^2}\frac{1}{b^3 v} \int_0^\pi \sin^4(\theta)\,d\theta\\ &= \frac{2e^6 Z^2}{3c^3 m^2}\frac{1}{b^3 v} \left(\frac{3\pi}{8}\right)\\ &= \boxed{\frac{\pi Z^2 e^6}{4m_e^2 c^3}\frac{1}{b^3 v}} \qquad \checkmark \end{align} $$

Part B

The emission angle for Cherenkov radiation is given by $$ \begin{align} \cos \theta = \frac{1}{n\beta} \end{align} $$ We want to find \(\beta\). A proton that has kinetic energy \(3760\) MeV will have total energy \(4700\) MeV. Then from \(E_\text{tot} = \gamma E_\text{rest}\) we can solve for \(\gamma\) to find $$ \begin{align} \gamma &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}} = \frac{4700}{940} \quad \implies \quad \beta = \frac{2\sqrt{6}}{5} \end{align} $$ Then the emission angle (for \(n = 1.5\)) is $$ \begin{align} \theta = \arccos\left(\frac{2}{3}\frac{5}{2\sqrt{6}}\right) \approx 47.12^\circ \end{align} $$
