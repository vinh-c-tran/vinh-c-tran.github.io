Problem 2

Starting with the “covariant” derivative of a vector \(V_i\) as $$ V_{i ; j} = \frac{\partial V_i}{\partial q^j} - V_k \Gamma^k_{ij} $$ where \( \Gamma^k_{ij} = \epsilon^k \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_i}{\partial q^j}\) and \(\epsilon\) denotes basis vectors, show that \(g_{ij;k}=0\) where \(g_{ij} =\epsilon_i \cdot \epsilon_j\) is the metric tensor. (Hint: use the) the product rule for derivatives on \(g_{ij}\)).

Solution

Generalizing the covariant derivative of a vector to a rank 2 tensor, we have the covariant derivative of the metric tensor given by \begin{align} g_{ij;k} &= \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial q^k} - g_{i\alpha}\Gamma^\alpha_{jk} - g_{j\beta}\Gamma^\beta_{ik} \label{eq1} \end{align} Next, we can use the product rule to re-write $\partial g_{ij}/\partial q^k$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial q^k} &= \frac{\partial (\epsilon_i \cdot \epsilon_j)}{\partial q^k} = \frac{\partial\epsilon_i}{\partial q^k}\cdot \epsilon_j + \epsilon_i \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_j}{\partial q^k} \end{align} Inserting this expression back into \ref{eq1} and re-writing the Christoffel symbols using $\Gamma_{ij}^k = \epsilon^k \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_i}{\partial q^j}$ gives us \begin{align} g_{ij;k} &= \frac{\partial\epsilon_i}{\partial q^k}\cdot \epsilon_j + \epsilon_i \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_j}{\partial q^k} - g_{i\alpha}\left[\epsilon^\alpha \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_j}{\partial q^k}\right] - g_{j\beta}\left[\epsilon^\beta \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_i}{\partial q^k}\right] \end{align} Then, we can use the lowering and raising properties of the metric tensor to re-write the last two terms as the first line. Then we see that the sum cancels out piece-wise. \begin{align} g_{ij;k} &= \frac{\partial\epsilon_i}{\partial q^k}\cdot \epsilon_j + \epsilon_i \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_j}{\partial q^k} - \epsilon_i \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_j}{\partial q^k} - \epsilon_j \cdot \frac{\partial \epsilon_i}{\partial q^k}\\ &= 0 \end{align}
